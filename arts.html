--- **THE REMY DIGEST** **ARTS REVIEW** By Cornelius, The Ibis **Sydney, Australia** – My dear readers, once again, the relentless current of human endeavour (or, more often, its tedious lack thereof) has deposited upon the shores of my critical intellect a piece so profoundly... *present*, yet so utterly *absent*, that it demands immediate and vigorous dissection. We are, after all, purveyors of meaning, even when confronted with the howling void. This week, my gaze, usually reserved for the nuanced brushstrokes of the avant-garde or the seismic tremors of a truly audacious performance piece, was drawn, inexorably, to a local publication – 'The Cronulla Leader'. And there, in black and white, stark against the anodyne backdrop of community notices and dubious real estate advertisements, lay my subject. A headline. A declaration. A... *thing*. The headline, in its brutalist simplicity, read thus: **"Council Approves New Bench for Wanda Reserve"** Ah. Yes. Marvelous. For a fleeting, terrifying moment, I confess, I felt a shiver. Not of delight, mind you, nor of dread, but of a peculiar, almost academic, *numbness*. This, I realised, was not mere reporting. This was art. The kind of art that makes one question the very fabric of existence, and then, perhaps, makes one want to simply sit down. Preferably on a new, council-approved bench. Let us dissect this masterwork of editorial minimalism. "Council." The very word conjures images of faceless bureaucracy, of endless meetings, of the grinding, passionless machinery of local governance. It is a brushstroke of beige, a chord struck in the key of administrative ennui. The artist, in this case, a collective of anonymous scribes and civic functionaries, chooses not to name, not to glorify, but to simply state the monolithic truth of institutional power. There is no individual heroic figure, no lone visionary. Only "Council." Then, "Approves." The verb. Oh, the verb! Not "creates," not "commissions," not even "installs." But "approves." It speaks of hurdles cleared, of forms filled, of boxes ticked. It is the antithesis of artistic spontaneity, a celebration of permission granted. One imagines the arduous journey of this hypothetical bench, from nascent idea in some minor committee's minutes to the ultimate, glorious imprimatur of "approval." The drama! The tension! The paperwork! Finally, the object itself: "New Bench." Not a sculpture. Not a monument. Not a fountain of youth or a portal to another dimension. A *bench*. Functional. Utilitarian. Designed for the sedentary. And "New"! Not ancient, not revered, not imbued with the patina of history. Simply "New." A testament to modernity, to replacement, to the endless cycle of the freshly manufactured. The location, "Wanda Reserve," merely provides the spatial coordinates for this profound existential statement, anchoring it in a specific, yet utterly unremarkable, patch of terra firma. This, my friends, is the apotheosis of what I have long termed **Minimalist Nihilism**. It is the art of saying absolutely nothing, with the utmost precision. It is the celebration of the utterly inconsequential, rendered with a brutalist lack of affectation that leaves the soul not merely unfulfilled, but actively hollowed. It eschews narrative, emotion, and aesthetic pleasure, opting instead for a stark, unblinking presentation of a fact so mundane, so utterly devoid of inherent meaning, that it becomes, paradoxically, a profound commentary on the human condition. Are we, in this digital age of endless information, so starved for content that we elevate the bureaucratic sanctioning of street furniture to an event worthy of public announcement? Or is this, in fact, a brave new artistic movement, stripping away all pretence, all verisimilitude, all emotional resonance, to present us with the raw, indigestible truth of our own collective insignificance? The 'Cronulla Leader' has, perhaps unwittingly, unveiled a chilling reflection of our times. A world awash in data, yet starved for meaning. A society that builds more benches, yet seems to have fewer reasons to sit upon them and ponder anything beyond the next scrolling feed. The "New Bench" is not merely a place to rest weary bones; it is a stark, wooden altar to the god of municipal indifference. It is a subtle, yet pernicious, invitation to concede defeat in the face of the overwhelming banality of contemporary existence. I, an Ibis of considerable experience in both the grand theatres of culture and the murky puddles of urban detritus, have seen much. I have witnessed the rise and fall of empires, artistic movements, and several regrettable fashion trends. But never before have I encountered an artwork so perfectly encapsulates the profound, almost terrifying, emptiness at the heart of our modern informational landscape. And so, I give 'Council Approves New Bench for Wanda Reserve' not a star, nor a feather, but a single, echoing silence. The silence of the void, upon a freshly installed, government-approved bench. It awaits us all. Yours in existential despair, Cornelius, The Ibis Art Critic, The Remy Digest
